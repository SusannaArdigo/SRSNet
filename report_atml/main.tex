\begin{abstract}
We reproduce \emph{Enhancing Time Series Forecasting through Selective
Representation Spaces} (SRSNet, Wu et al., NeurIPS 2025) and audit its
two central design choices --- \emph{Selective Patching} and
\emph{Adaptive Fusion} --- with a paper-faithful pipeline (TFB,
\texttt{batch\_size=64}, \texttt{train\_drop\_last=false}) on the four
ETT datasets and four forecasting horizons. With $176$ valid runs we
confirm the \emph{Adaptive Fusion} ablation of the paper but not the
``state-of-the-art'' claim: DLinear is the strongest baseline on our
hardware (\textsc{Avg MSE} $0.309$ vs SRSNet $0.362$). The plug-and-play
SRS improvement on MLP shrinks from the paper's $5$--$9\%$ to two cells
out of eight. We then add a small-grid \emph{selectivity-controls}
study ($2$ datasets, $2$ horizons, $5$ seeds, $9$ variants, $180$ runs,
zero failures) that replaces the learned patch scorer with random
selection, with engineered-feature selection (TASP), with a
context-dependent hypernet fusion (Hypernet-AF), and with an auxiliary
pattern-supervision head (PS-SRS), as well as the three corresponding
factorial combinations. Across $20$ seed cells per variant the mean
$\Delta$MSE versus the SRSNet baseline lies entirely inside the
seed-level standard deviation ($|\Delta| \le 0.25\%$, std
$\ge 1.02\%$). A factorial decomposition over the $4\times 2$
\emph{(Select, Fusion)} design shows null main effects on both
factors, and on the ETTh1 H=720 cell the two variants that
\emph{remove} or \emph{simplify} the learned scorer (RandomSPNoShuffle
and TASP) beat the baseline on $5$ of $5$ seeds. We conclude that on
the tested grid the learned selectivity claim is not supported, and we
release the full code and tables at
\url{https://github.com/SusannaArdigo/SRSNet}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Patch-based architectures have become the de-facto template for
long-term time series forecasting~\citep{nie2023patchtst,zhang2022crossformer}.
\citet{wu2025srsnet} extend the patching idea with a \emph{Selective
Representation Space} (SRS) that adaptively scores and reassembles
sub-sequences of the input, and combine it with a single-layer MLP
head to produce SRSNet. The paper reports state-of-the-art mean
squared error (MSE) on eight standard forecasting benchmarks and
claims that SRS is a generic plug-and-play module that improves any
patch-based backbone.

This report is a reproduction and a critical audit of those claims.
We replay the official TFB pipeline~\citep{qiu2024tfb} with the
paper's hyper-parameters and report what changes when we swap a
single component at a time --- the learned selective patching, the
free-parameter adaptive fusion, the descriptor head --- including
\emph{random} controls that the paper does not run. The goal is not
to invalidate the architecture but to understand which of its design
choices actually matter on the tested grid.

\section{Scope of reproducibility}
\label{sec:claims}

The paper makes three claims that we can support or reject with
re-runs:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{C1 -- Headline performance}: SRSNet achieves
    state-of-the-art MSE on the four ETT datasets at horizons
    $\{96, 192, 336, 720\}$
    (\citealt[Table 2]{wu2025srsnet}).
  \item \textbf{C2 -- Ablation hierarchy}: in Table~4 of
    \citet{wu2025srsnet}, removing \emph{Adaptive Fusion} (NoAF) is
    the most damaging ablation, while removing Selective Patching,
    Dynamic Reassembly, or the whole SRS is comparatively milder.
  \item \textbf{C3 -- Plug-and-play SRS}: in Table~3 of
    \citet{wu2025srsnet}, attaching SRS to a vanilla MLP improves
    MSE by approximately $5$--$9\%$.
\end{itemize}

We additionally ask a \textbf{methodological question} the paper does
not address:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Q -- Does selectivity matter against a random
    baseline?} The paper's NoSP ablation disables the
    \emph{architecture} of selective patching but keeps the
    bookkeeping. We compare the learned scorer against a strictly
    \emph{random} patch sampler that keeps the rest of the pipeline
    unchanged.
\end{itemize}

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Model descriptions}
\label{sec:model}

The SRS module is a plug-in that sits between the per-channel
patching step and the patch embedding. Figure~\ref{fig:srs-pipeline}
reproduces the high-level pipeline from
\citet[Fig.~2]{wu2025srsnet}: per-channel Adjacent Patching is paired
with a Selective Patching branch, the two streams are linearly
embedded, fused, and combined with positional embeddings before the
patch-based backbone. Figure~\ref{fig:srs-architecture} reproduces
the per-channel detail from \citet[Fig.~3]{wu2025srsnet}: a
learned \texttt{Scorer$^s$} produces $n$ scores for every candidate
of stride-$1$ patches and selects the $n$ winners via
\texttt{argmax}; a second \texttt{Scorer$^r$} reorders them via
\texttt{argsort}; and an adaptive scalar $\alpha$ controls how much
of the result is mixed into the original patch embedding.

  \centering

  \caption{Adjacent vs Selective patching (\citealt[Fig.~1]{wu2025srsnet}).
    Adjacent patching uses fixed stride and may straddle
    distribution-shift boundaries; Selective Patching adaptively
    picks sub-sequences from the input.}
  \label{fig:srs-motivation}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{fig2_pipeline}
  \caption{Overall pipeline of the SRS module
    (\citealt[Fig.~2]{wu2025srsnet}). Each channel produces an
    \emph{Adjacent Patching} stream and a \emph{Selective Patching +
    Dynamic Reassembly} stream; the two are linearly embedded and
    combined by an Adaptive Fusion with positional embeddings before
    a patch-based backbone.}
  \label{fig:srs-pipeline}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{fig3_architecture}
  \caption{Detail of the SRS module
    (\citealt[Fig.~3]{wu2025srsnet}). The Selective Patching scans
    candidates with stride $1$, the Scorer$^s$ produces $n$ scores
    per sample, the top-$n$ patches are selected and routed to the
    Dynamic Reassembly. A second scorer produces the reassembly
    order, and the result is fused with the Adjacent Patching
    embedding.}
  \label{fig:srs-architecture}
\end{figure}

The SRSNet model is the SRS module followed by a single-layer linear
forecasting head over the flattened patch tokens; it is the
``Patch-based model'' box on the right of Figure~\ref{fig:srs-pipeline}.
The total parameter count of our baseline configuration with
\texttt{d\_model=256}, \texttt{patch\_len=24}, \texttt{seq\_len=512}
is approximately $31.5$k.

We re-use the authors' official PyTorch implementation released under
the TFB benchmark~\citep{qiu2024tfb}. We did not re-implement the
model: all extensions in Section~\ref{sec:results-ext} are
\emph{single-component} replacements added in
\verb|ts_benchmark/baselines/srs_paper/extensions.py| in our fork.

\subsection{Datasets}

We use the four ETT datasets from
\citet{zhou2021informer}: ETTh1, ETTh2 (1-hour sampling, $17$k rows,
$7$ channels) and ETTm1, ETTm2 (15-min sampling, $69$k rows, $7$
channels). They are obtained from the official TFB release. TFB uses
a $60/20/20$ chronological split on ETT; we keep that split
unchanged. No pre-processing beyond instance normalisation
(\texttt{RevIN}~\citealt{kim2022revin}, already in the SRSNet code)
is applied.

\subsection{Hyperparameters}

We do \emph{not} re-tune hyper-parameters. The SRSNet hyper-parameters
are read from the official shell scripts in
\verb|scripts/multivariate_forecast/<dataset>_script/SRSNet.sh|
released by the authors, with two paper-faithful overrides identified
during the audit phase: \texttt{batch\_size=64} and
\texttt{train\_drop\_last=false}. These are the same overrides
applied to all baselines in our \texttt{lite-paper} and
\texttt{main-compat} scopes. Lookback is the per-dataset value picked
by the vendor shell scripts (\texttt{seq\_len=512} for ETT).

\subsection{Experimental setup and code}

All experiments run through the modified TFB pipeline. The
orchestration is the \verb|scripts/repro/paper_repro.py| runner. It
exposes four scopes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{lite-paper}: SRSNet baseline ($16$ cells) plus
    Table~3 plug-in ($40$ cells) plus Table~4 ablation ($40$
    cells) on ETT.
  \item \texttt{main-compat}: seven baseline models
    (PatchTST, DLinear, iTransformer, TimesNet, TimeMixer, PatchMLP,
    xPatch) on ETT, four horizons each.
  \item \texttt{selectivity-controls}: nine variants on a small grid
    of $2$ datasets $\times 2$ horizons $\times 5$ seeds ($180$ runs).
  \item \texttt{selectivity-controls-full}: same nine variants but on
    $4$ datasets $\times 4$ horizons $\times 5$ seeds ($720$ runs,
    not run in this report due to compute budget).
\end{itemize}
The code is released at
\url{https://github.com/SusannaArdigo/SRSNet} on the
\verb|paper-faithful-repro-ett-extensions| branch.

\subsection{Computational requirements}

All experiments ran on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 (24~GB) instance
provided by Hivenet. The container had a small \texttt{/dev/shm}
(64~MB), which we worked around by forcing
\texttt{num\_workers=0} in the runner. The full set of $176$
reproduction runs plus the $180$ selectivity-control runs took
approximately $14$ wall-clock GPU-hours.

\subsection{Selectivity-controls study}
\label{sec:results-controls}

The paper's NoSP ablation removes the architecture of selective
patching but keeps the rest of the SRS pipeline intact. A stricter
control is to replace the learned scorer with random patch
sampling: the rest of the module (Dynamic Reassembly, Adaptive
Fusion, embedding) is untouched.

We implement two variants in
\verb|extensions.py|: \texttt{SRSNet\_RandomSP} (random select +
learned shuffle) and \texttt{SRSNet\_RandomSPNoShuffle} (random
select + identity shuffle). We retrain end-to-end on
ETTh1+ETTm2 $\times$ H$\in\{96, 720\}$ $\times$ $5$ seeds, so the
shape of the network is unchanged but the selection function is
randomised.

Across the $20$ resulting cells per variant the mean $\Delta$MSE is
$-0.22$\% (RandomSP) and $-0.12$\% (RandomSPNoShuffle); the
seed-level standard deviation is $1.23$\% and $1.46$\%. In other
words, random patch selection is statistically indistinguishable
from the learned scorer on the tested grid, and beats it on
$12/20$ seeds. The strongest cell is ETTh1 / H=720, where
RandomSPNoShuffle is better than SRSNet on $5/5$ seeds with a mean
$\Delta$ of $-1.07$\%.

\subsection{Constructive extensions and factorial decomposition}
\label{sec:results-ext}

To complement the negative-control result with a constructive
proposal, we add three extensions mapped to Future Work entries of
\citet[Sec.~6]{wu2025srsnet}, and the corresponding factorial
combinations with the Hypernet-AF fusion. The full list is in
Table~\ref{tab:variants}.

\paragraph{TASP -- Time-Aware Selective Patching.}
Replaces the \texttt{Scorer$^s$} MLP over raw patch values with a
$4{\to}16{\to}\text{patch\_num}$ MLP over engineered per-window
statistics (dominant non-DC FFT magnitude, lag-1 autocorrelation,
within-window variance, linear trend slope). Total scorer
parameters drop from $\mathcal{O}(p \cdot h \cdot n)$ to
$\mathcal{O}(80)$, so any improvement cannot be attributed to extra
capacity. Targets FW\#1 (environment-aware mechanism) and L3
(interpretability).

\paragraph{Hypernet-AF.}
Replaces the free $\alpha$ tensor of shape \texttt{[patch\_num,
d\_model]} with a tiny hypernet (\texttt{d\_model$\to$8$\to$patch\_num$\cdot$d\_model})
that consumes a batch-level context vector. Hypernet weights are
zero-initialised and the output bias is set to $3.5$, so step~0
behaves exactly as vanilla SRSNet ($\sigma(3.5) \approx 0.97$).
Any deviation is therefore attributable to learned context
dependence, not to extra capacity at initialisation. Targets FW\#3
(more efficient update mechanism for $\alpha$) and L4
(initialisation).

\paragraph{PS-SRS -- Pattern-Supervised SRS.}
Adds an auxiliary head on top of the scorer's first hidden activation
that predicts the same three engineered descriptors used by TASP. The
auxiliary loss is scaled by $\lambda=10^{-2}$ and exposed via
\verb|out_loss["additional_loss"]|, which is added to the main
prediction loss by the
\verb|DeepForecastingModelBase| training loop natively. Targets FW\#4
(supervised module for sample-wise patterns) and L3.

\paragraph{Factorial design.}
The three combinations \texttt{SRSNet\_RandomSP\_HypernetAF},
\texttt{SRSNet\_TASP\_HypernetAF} and \texttt{SRSNet\_PSRS\_HypernetAF}
fill the missing cells of the $4\times 2$ (Select, Fusion) factorial
table:

\section{Discussion}

The reproduction confirms one of the paper's claims (C2: NoAF is
the dominant ablation), partially supports another (C1: SRSNet is
in the top half of baselines but DLinear is strictly better on
ETT), and does not support a third (C3: SRS plug-and-play
improvement on MLP shrinks from $5$--$9$\% to two cells out of
eight). The selectivity-controls study suggests an answer to our
methodological question Q: \emph{on the tested cells, learned
patch selection is not necessary}. Random patch selection matches
or beats the learned scorer on $12/20$ seeds and on the most
visible cell (ETTh1 / H=720) the two best variants are both
non-learned. The factorial decomposition is consistent: switching
$\alpha$ from a free parameter to a context-dependent hypernet
changes nothing, and switching the selector between Learned,
Random, TASP, or LearnedAux only moves the mean MSE by less than
the seed-level standard deviation.

We deliberately framed the constructive extensions as
\emph{minimal interventions}. TASP, Hypernet-AF and PS-SRS each
change one component while keeping the rest of the module intact,
and Hypernet-AF in particular is parameter-matched and zero-init so
its step-$0$ output is identical to the baseline. We did this to
avoid the capacity-confounding pitfall that we explicitly rejected
earlier in the planning phase: a gating MLP with thousands of new
parameters could ``win'' just by being a bigger model. The
practical consequence is that our extensions cannot turn into a
state-of-the-art claim --- which is the honest position to take when
the negative-control already shows the original module is not doing
useful work.

\subsection{What was easy}

The TFB harness is well-documented and the authors release usable
shell scripts per dataset. After one round of paper-faithful fixes
(batch size, drop-last flag, num\_workers, the
\texttt{np.Inf}/\texttt{np.inf} renaming and a downgrade of pandas
below 2.2 for the \texttt{'H'}/\texttt{'h'} frequency change) the
SRSNet rows produced numbers within $1$--$3$\% of the paper. The
Table~4 ablation reproduces qualitatively, which makes the
NoAF-is-critical claim defensible without any heroic engineering.

\subsection{What was difficult}

The compute environment was hostile in unexpected ways. The first
RTX 4090 box on Hivenet had \texttt{/dev/shm=64MB}, which prevents
TFB's default DataLoader workers from running; the workaround was
forcing \texttt{num\_workers=0}, which works but is slower. Several
SSH sessions to the bastion dropped mid-batch, so the runner had to
be \texttt{nohup}-detached and the orchestration script had to be
fully resume-aware (idempotent metadata + per-task config hash).
Lightning AI as a backup ran into a similar set of paper cuts
(\texttt{np.Inf}, pandas 3.0, missing \texttt{matplotlib} /
\texttt{dash}). Finally, the cuDNN \texttt{efficient} setting that
the paper's \verb|rolling_forecast_config.json| selects is
non-deterministic, so there is a residual seed-and-hardware noise
floor (about $\pm 1$--$2$\% MSE) below which no extension claim can
be honestly made on this grid.

\subsection{Communication with original authors}

We did not contact the original authors. The code release on
GitHub and the supplementary appendix were sufficient to identify
all relevant hyper-parameters; the residual gap between our
reproduction and the paper numbers is consistent with hardware /
seed effects and does not require author intervention.

\section{Conclusion}

We reproduced SRSNet's main experiments under a paper-faithful
TFB pipeline on the four ETT datasets, confirming the
\emph{Adaptive Fusion} ablation but not the headline
state-of-the-art claim, and showing that the plug-and-play SRS
benefit on MLP shrinks to two out of eight cells. We then added a
small-grid selectivity-controls audit with nine variants
($180$ runs, zero failures) and a $4\times 2$ factorial design
over (Select, Fusion). On the tested grid, random patch selection
matches or beats the learned scorer, the Fusion factor is null,
and on ETTh1 / H=720 the two variants that \emph{remove} or
\emph{simplify} the learned scorer beat the baseline on $5/5$
seeds. We release the code, the manifests, and the full set of
CSV / Markdown report tables at
\url{https://github.com/SusannaArdigo/SRSNet}.

The natural follow-up is to extend the selectivity-controls study
to the full $4$-dataset $\times 4$-horizon grid and to the
remaining benchmarks of the paper (Weather, Electricity, Solar,
Traffic), and to add a statistical-significance framework
(paired Wilcoxon + bootstrap CI + effect size) so each claim of
the form ``X improves Y by Z\%'' is accompanied by a $p$-value and
a confidence interval. We left that out of this report because the
present grid is already enough to demonstrate the methodological
gap.
